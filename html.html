What experience do you have working with datasets related to housing, income, or demographics?

I worked as a Statistical Researcher at the General Authority of Statistics (GSTAT) from 2019 to 2021. My responsibilities went beyond the Energy Department, where I collaborated with colleagues to routinely quality-control, analyze, and publish data from surveys such as the Annual Household Energy Consumption Survey. I also helped design and implement the Transportation Sector Energy Consumption Survey from the ground up. In addition, I contributed to major national projects, including the 2019 Labor Force Survey and the Saudi Census 2020, through collecting, controlling data. All GSTAT surveys incorporated detailed demographic information such as age, household size, education, income, housing characteristics, and ownership type. This gave me experience working with large-scale demographic, income, and housing datasets

Have you used tools like Excel, R, Stata, SAS, or Python for data analysis? If so, for what kinds of projects?

During my time at GSTAT, I became familiar with a wide range of statistical tools. I excelled in Power BI for data visualization and reporting, participated in workshops for R, and began developing skills in Python. I also gained working knowledge of SPSS and earlier experience with EViews. In Spring 2025, I had a major shift in my R proficiency through the Econometrics Forecasting course, where R was used extensively for time-series modeling and forecasting. This semester, in Statistical Computation, I am further strengthening my skills in R and Python, while also learning complementary tools such as HTML, which I am currently applying to build this website

In 2017, the Trump Administration created the Opportunity Zone program to help revitalize low-income areas across the U.S. by providing tax incentives to investors that invest in local businesses or real estate through Qualified Opportunity Funds. How would you go about researching the impact of the program on the Texas housing market?

Since the question is about the impact on the Texas housing sector, and these zones represent an expansionary policy expected to stimulate real estate developers to increase investment, I would focus on a range of housing-related datasets, including construction materials demand, supply, and prices, as well as employment level and contracts in the construction sector. I would also analyze building permits, exchanged property deeds, housing demand, supply, and prices, along with indicators of commercial activity in these zones. Finally, I would compare the updated data with the historical time series of each variable, and with non-opportunitiy zone areas.

However, when I searched about that, updated by Jan 2021 studies show Opportunity Zone investments have been highly concentrated in a few urban areas, with mixed or limited effects on housing, jobs, and local economic activity (Tax Policy Center, What are Opportunity Zones and how do they work?)

There is a lot of speculation about whether the home prices will crash. How would you go about researching what a crash is and whether we are heading towards one?

In general, a crash can be defined as an unusual and sustained decrease in prices over a relatively long period, such as the housing bubble of 2008. To study whether we are heading toward such a crash, I would examine datasets and variables, including variables I mentioned previously, alongside broader global, national, and local indicators, including interest rates, mortgage credit availability, insurance costs, and employment trends. Additionally, I would apply trusted forecasting models to evaluate potential crashes and risks. As Sabrina Parsons, CEO of LivePlan, says, ‚ÄúAccurate is different from right. A good forecast needs to represent your business, not predict the future.‚Äù
